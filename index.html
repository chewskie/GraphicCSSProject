Birth Rate Declines With Greater Access To Contraception,

But Are We Also Dissatisfied With The World?

By Sophie Janus

October 3 2021

"I would be scared to have a child right now, I’m not gonna lie. A lot of people I know are not in good situations so having a child is seen as a burden instead of something beautiful.”

- Janine, 28, Brooklyn

This past Spring the National Center for Health Statistics reported that in 2020 3.6 million babies were born in the United States. The year before, 3.75 million babies were born, indicating a 4% drop in birth rate. This marks the sixth consecutive year that the number of births has declined, down an average of 2% per year. Last year’s double drop signals that the pandemic specifically is causing some American women to either delay pregnancy or not have children at all.

This shift is reflected worldwide. According to the UN World Population Prospects statistics, the current birth rate globally in 2021 is 17.873 births per 1000 people, a 1.13% decline from 2020, which respectively saw a 1.12 % decline from 2019. The global fertility rate declined from 3.2 live births per woman in 1990 to 2.5 in 2019. A 2020 report done by the UN predicts that fertility is expected to continue declining. The global level of fertility is expected to reach 2.2 live births per woman in 2050 and 1.9 in 2100.

There is a positive correlation between declining birth rate and access to contraception. Globally, in 2019, 49% of all women in the reproductive age range (15-49 years) were using some form of contraception, an increase from 42% in 1990.

The data further demonstrates that 50% of the population lives in a country or area where lifetime fertility is below 2.1 live births per woman, which is estimated to be the level required for populations with low mortality to have a growth rate of zero long term.

All this data shows that more women are choosing, or are increasingly able to choose, when and if they want to have kids. However, the UN statistics do not illustrate why exactly people are choosing to have fewer children, or to have none at all.

A 2018 study done by the New York Times asked young adults in the US to list the factors that influenced them to have, or to plan on having, less children than they considered ideal. The most common listed influential factor was that childcare is too expensive (64% of the 1858 people interviewed).

Descending listed factors by order of popularity included wanting to have more time for the children they already had (54%), worry about the economy (49%), inability to afford more children (44%)...

...prolonged waiting due to financial instability (43%), and wanting more leisure time (42%)...

...Other answers included worry about global instability (37%), worry about climate change (33%), worry about population growth (27%), prioritization of education and career (23%), and the belief that they would be an unfit parent (13%).

Sources:

  • UN World Population Prospects Statistics, accessed by Macro Trends and the UN World Fertility and Family Planning Highlights 2020
  • New York Times study conducted by Morning Consult
